# Crisis Communications

**Pillar · Crisis Communications**

Coverage of how organizations prepare for, respond to, and recover from crises — across product, financial, regulatory, cybersecurity, executive, and reputational events that can compress significant brand value into a single news cycle.

## What is Crisis Communications?

Crisis communications is the discipline of managing public-facing communication during events that threaten organizational reputation, market position, or stakeholder trust. The practice covers preparation (scenario planning, dark-site infrastructure, designated spokespeople, holding statements), real-time response (statement strategy, media handling, stakeholder briefings, social channels), and recovery (post-event reputation rebuilding, narrative reset, regulatory follow-through). The discipline overlaps with reputation management, public affairs, legal communications, and executive communications.

The modern practice took shape through a series of formative cases. Johnson & Johnson's response to the 1982 Tylenol cyanide poisonings is widely cited as the foundation of contemporary crisis communications methodology — transparency, immediate consumer protection, and proactive media engagement. The 1989 Exxon Valdez oil spill demonstrated the cost of delayed and defensive response. The 2010 Deepwater Horizon disaster reshaped expectations around CEO visibility during crises. The 2017 United Airlines passenger-removal incident illustrated how social-media-era crises compress on a different timeline than broadcast-era crises. The current period adds cybersecurity disclosure, deepfake and synthetic-media risk, regulator-driven cascading crises, and AI-mediated reputational summaries that persist long after a news cycle ends.

## How is the Market Changing?

Crises now move faster, surface across more channels simultaneously, and persist longer in public reference. A single triggering event — a leaked document, a social-media post, a regulatory filing, a cyberattack disclosure — can produce parallel news cycles across broadcast, trade press, social platforms, and generative search interfaces within hours. The window between event and required response has compressed. Stakeholder expectations have widened: customers, employees, regulators, investors, and creator-economy commentators now all expect communication targeted to them.

A secondary shift: the persistence layer. AI-mediated summaries of past crises tend to reflect the dominant editorial framing of the event for years after the news cycle closes. Organizations that managed the initial response well — clear, primary-source, on-the-record communication — generally show up better in those downstream summaries than organizations that managed the response defensively.

## Why Preparation Matters

Crises that organizations have prepared for generally cost meaningfully less to manage than crises that organizations encounter without preparation. Preparation includes documented scenarios, pre-drafted holding statements, designated spokespeople (with current media training), pre-built dark-site infrastructure, escalation protocols, legal and HR coordination, and standing relationships with the press corps covering the organization. The pre-event infrastructure determines how the first 24 hours play out. The first 24 hours typically shape the framing for the entire news cycle.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is crisis communications?**
Crisis communications is the discipline of managing public-facing communication during events that threaten organizational reputation, market position, or stakeholder trust. The practice covers preparation, real-time response, and post-event recovery, and overlaps with reputation management, public affairs, legal communications, and executive communications.

**What is the first 24 hours of a crisis playbook?**
The first 24 hours typically involve event verification and internal fact-gathering, escalation to designated decision-makers, drafting and approval of a holding statement, coordinated response across press, social, employee, and regulator channels, and the activation of pre-built infrastructure (dark sites, spokespeople, legal coordination). The objective in the first 24 hours is generally to establish a credible initial position, demonstrate organizational responsibility where appropriate, and prevent the news cycle from being framed entirely by outside parties.

**How have crisis communications changed in the digital era?**
Three structural shifts. First, the timeline has compressed — crises now surface across multiple channels simultaneously within hours rather than developing over a 24-hour news cycle. Second, the stakeholder set has widened to include creator-economy commentators, employee activists, and social-platform audiences alongside traditional press. Third, the persistence layer has grown — AI-generated summaries of past crises tend to reflect the dominant editorial framing for years after the news cycle closes.

**Should organizations prepare for crises before they happen?**
Most large organizations now treat crisis preparation as a standing function rather than an event-driven activity. Standard preparation includes documented scenarios, pre-drafted holding statements, designated and media-trained spokespeople, dark-site infrastructure, escalation protocols, and coordinated legal and HR procedures. Organizations that have invested in preparation generally manage events more effectively than organizations that have not.

**How do AI and deepfakes change crisis response?**
Two implications. First, synthetic media — fabricated audio, video, or documents — has become a new triggering event for crises, and organizations are now building verification and response infrastructure for these scenarios. Second, AI-generated summaries of past events shape ongoing reputational reference, which has increased the strategic importance of getting the initial response right and producing high-quality primary-source content during and after the event.

## What is Influencer Marketing?

Influencer marketing is the discipline of building brand presence through partnerships with content creators who maintain audience relationships across social, video, and audio platforms. The practice spans paid sponsorships, gifted product seeding, ambassador programs, creator-led commerce, affiliate structures, and increasingly long-term creator-as-strategic-partner arrangements. The discipline overlaps with social media, public relations, content marketing, and paid media.

Celebrity endorsement has a century-plus precedent in consumer marketing. The modern creator-economy version of the discipline emerged with the rise of YouTube (founded 2005), Instagram (2010), and the formalization of brand-creator partnerships through the 2010s. The U.S. Federal Trade Commission introduced endorsement-disclosure guidelines in 2009 and updated them substantially in 2013, 2017, and 2023, formalizing the rules around sponsored content. TikTok's emergence after 2018 reshaped creator economics, audience-discovery dynamics, and the tier structure that now defines the industry. The medium continues to expand across Twitch, LinkedIn (for B2B creator work), Substack, YouTube, and Reddit.

## How is the Market Changing?

The creator-economy category has continued to expand. Goldman Sachs estimated the global market at roughly $250 billion in 2024. The tier structure has matured into a more granular hierarchy — mega-creators (10 million+ followers), macro-creators (500,000 to 10 million), micro-creators (50,000 to 500,000), and nano-creators (under 50,000) — with sponsorship economics, engagement patterns, and brand-fit considerations differing significantly across tiers.

Three additional shifts. First, the relationship model has evolved from one-off paid posts toward long-term creator-brand partnerships, with measurable performance differences between the two. Second, regulatory disclosure enforcement has tightened, particularly in beauty, finance, and supplements. Third, AI-generated content disclosure has become a developing regulatory area, with the European Union introducing explicit requirements ahead of the U.S.

## Why Creator Partnerships Compound

Creators with established audience trust generally produce higher engagement and stronger brand-attribution effects than equivalent paid placements. Long-term creator partnerships tend to outperform one-off sponsored posts in both immediate and durable measurement. Creator content also produces second-order distribution — through reposting, social-clip libraries, AI-mediated retrieval of creator commentary, and the broader cultural conversation creators help shape.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is influencer marketing?**
Influencer marketing is the discipline of building brand presence through partnerships with content creators across social, video, and audio platforms. The practice spans paid sponsorships, gifted product seeding, ambassador programs, creator-led commerce, and long-term creator-brand partnerships. Goldman Sachs estimated the global creator-economy market at roughly $250 billion in 2024.

**What is the creator tier structure?**
The industry generally categorizes creators into four tiers based on audience size: mega-creators (10 million+ followers), macro-creators (500,000 to 10 million), micro-creators (50,000 to 500,000), and nano-creators (under 50,000). Sponsorship economics, engagement patterns, and brand-fit considerations vary significantly across tiers. Micro and nano creators frequently produce higher engagement rates than larger creators, though with smaller absolute reach.

**How does FTC disclosure work?**
The U.S. Federal Trade Commission requires creators to clearly disclose material connections to brands — including paid partnerships, gifted products, and affiliate relationships. Disclosure must be conspicuous and unavoidable. The current guidelines (most recently updated in 2023) cover sponsored content across social platforms, livestreams, and increasingly AI-generated content. Enforcement has tightened in beauty, supplements, and financial services.

**Do creator partnerships work for B2B?**
Yes, increasingly. LinkedIn has become a substantial B2B creator platform, with senior practitioners, executives, and category specialists operating as influencers within their fields. YouTube and Substack host significant B2B creator audiences in technology, finance, and policy. The mechanics differ from consumer creator work — audiences are smaller, intent is higher, and sponsorship structures favor long-term content partnerships over one-off posts.

**How is influencer marketing different from PR?**
Public relations historically operates through earned third-party media — press, journalists, and editorial coverage. Influencer marketing primarily operates through paid or gifted creator partnerships. The two disciplines increasingly overlap as creators have taken on functions that resembled traditional press coverage — product reviews, category commentary, and audience advocacy — and as brands have built integrated programs that span both.

## What is B2B Marketing?

B2B marketing is the discipline of marketing to organizations rather than to individual consumers. The practice spans demand generation, account-based marketing (ABM), trade media relations, analyst relations, product marketing, partner marketing, field marketing, and the increasingly integrated set of digital and content disciplines feeding enterprise buying-committee research.

The discipline traces from direct-mail and trade-publication marketing in the early 20th century. The postwar era saw the rise of trade shows and field sales as central B2B functions. Marketing automation emerged in the late 1990s and 2000s (Eloqua, founded 1999; Marketo, 2006; HubSpot, 2006), enabling lead-scoring and pipeline-management at scale. The 2010s formalized account-based marketing — replacing or supplementing volume lead-generation with targeted campaigns aimed at specific named accounts. The current period integrates intent-data platforms, AI-assisted research by buying committees, and the role of generative search interfaces in vendor shortlisting.

## How is the Market Changing?

Enterprise buying committees have expanded. Most large B2B purchases now involve six to ten internal stakeholders across procurement, technical, financial, and end-user functions. Research happens across more channels — trade press, analyst reports, peer review sites (G2, TrustRadius, Gartner Peer Insights), vendor websites, LinkedIn commentary, podcasts, and increasingly conversational AI interfaces — before vendors are ever contacted directly.

Three structural shifts. First, the share of the buying journey completed before sales contact has continued to grow. Second, AI-generated category summaries are now part of early-stage research for an expanding share of buying committees, which has elevated the strategic importance of analyst inclusion, earned media coverage, and the structured editorial content that AI systems draw on. Third, the boundary between B2B marketing and B2B public relations has blurred — most mature programs now run earned, owned, paid, and analyst work as a single integrated function.

## Why Distinctive Editorial and Analyst Coverage Matter

Enterprise buyers and the AI systems they consult both favor vendors with substantive editorial presence — analyst inclusion, primary-source research, named executive commentary, structured comparison content, and consistent trade-press coverage. Vendors that have invested in those signals tend to enter consideration sets earlier and stay in them longer than vendors that have relied primarily on outbound demand generation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is B2B marketing?**
B2B marketing is the discipline of marketing to organizations — enterprise and SMB — rather than to individual consumers. The practice covers demand generation, account-based marketing, trade media relations, analyst relations, product marketing, partner marketing, field marketing, and the digital and content disciplines feeding enterprise buying-committee research.

**How is B2B marketing different from B2C?**
Most B2B purchases involve buying committees of six to ten stakeholders across procurement, technical, financial, and end-user functions. Sales cycles are typically longer (weeks to multiple years rather than days), deal sizes are higher, and the research process is more research-intensive — drawing on analyst frameworks, trade media, peer review sites, and increasingly AI-generated category summaries. The marketing tactics adapted to this environment look meaningfully different from consumer marketing tactics.

**What is account-based marketing (ABM)?**
ABM is a B2B marketing approach that targets specific named accounts with coordinated, account-specific campaigns rather than broad lead-generation. The practice formalized in the mid-2010s and has expanded into a substantial software and services category. ABM programs typically combine intent data, targeted advertising, personalized content, and coordinated sales-and-marketing outreach against accounts the vendor has identified as high-priority.

**How do AI systems influence B2B buying?**
Generative search interfaces are now part of early-stage research for an expanding share of buying committees. Buyers ask AI systems to summarize categories, compare vendors, and recommend shortlists. The summaries draw on analyst content, trade press, vendor websites, peer review sites, and editorial coverage — meaning vendors with substantive presence across those sources tend to appear more frequently in AI-generated category summaries than vendors without.

**What's the relationship between B2B marketing and B2B PR?**
The boundary has blurred. Most mature programs now run earned media, owned content, paid demand generation, and analyst relations as a single integrated function. The disciplines remain distinct in execution — earned media requires journalist relationships, ABM requires intent-data infrastructure, analyst relations requires structured briefing programs — but they increasingly share leadership, budget, and measurement frameworks.

## What is Paid Media?

Paid media is the discipline of purchasing audience attention through advertising — across search, social platforms, programmatic display, connected TV, retail media networks, audio (podcasts and streaming), broadcast, print, out-of-home, and emerging surfaces including in-game and AI search advertising. The practice spans media planning, media buying, creative development, performance measurement, and the technical operations of running campaigns across an increasingly complex platform landscape.

The modern paid-media industry took shape through several phases. Search advertising emerged with the launch of Google AdWords in 2000, establishing the auction-based, performance-attribution model that has reshaped advertising economics. The programmatic display era began in the late 2000s and accelerated through the 2010s, automating the buying and selling of digital impressions. Social platform monetization followed — Facebook Ads (rolled out across the late 2000s), LinkedIn, Twitter, and the more recent dominance of Meta, TikTok, and YouTube. The 2020s have introduced connected-TV growth, the retail-media network category (Amazon Ads, Walmart Connect, Target Roundel, Kroger Precision Marketing), the unwinding of third-party cookies, and the early commercial development of AI-search advertising.

## How is the Market Changing?

Three structural shifts. First, the retail-media network category has grown rapidly, with eMarketer estimating U.S. retail media ad spend at roughly $60 billion in 2024. Second, connected TV (CTV) has continued to take share from traditional linear television, reshaping how brands plan video advertising. Third, the long-anticipated deprecation of third-party cookies has progressed unevenly — Google reversed its full deprecation plan in 2024, leaving the industry in a mixed-signal environment where identity, attribution, and measurement frameworks are all in transition.

A fourth, more recent shift: AI-search interfaces have begun introducing sponsored placement formats. ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google's AI Overviews are at various stages of commercial development around advertising integration. The category is small relative to traditional search but is widely expected to expand as conversational interfaces capture a larger share of high-intent research.

## Why Measurement and Attribution Are Under Pressure

The combination of partial cookie deprecation, platform-level identity restrictions (Apple's App Tracking Transparency framework, browser tracking limitations), and the multi-channel research patterns of modern buyers has put pressure on traditional click-and-conversion attribution. Marketing mix modeling (MMM) has returned as a significant measurement discipline. Incrementality testing has expanded. Cross-channel attribution remains contested — and most sophisticated programs now use multiple measurement approaches rather than relying on any single attribution model.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is paid media?**
Paid media is the discipline of purchasing audience attention through advertising — across search, social, programmatic display, connected TV, retail media networks, audio, broadcast, print, and out-of-home. And the cookie-deprecation transition has progressed unevenly, leaving identity, attribution, and measurement frameworks in a mixed-signal state. **What are retail media networks?**
Retail media networks are advertising platforms operated by major retailers — Amazon Ads, Walmart Connect, Target Roundel, Kroger Precision Marketing, and similar — that allow brands to advertise to shoppers using the retailer's first-party customer data. The category has grown rapidly because of the combination of high-intent audience, closed-loop measurement, and the closing-off of third-party tracking elsewhere in the ecosystem.

**Why is marketing mix modeling back?**
Marketing mix modeling (MMM) is a statistical approach to measuring the contribution of marketing channels to business outcomes using aggregated data rather than user-level tracking. MMM predates the digital era but has returned as a significant measurement discipline because the partial deprecation of third-party cookies and the platform-level identity restrictions have reduced the reliability of click-and-conversion attribution.

## What is Event & Experiential Marketing?

Event and experiential marketing is the discipline of designing live and hybrid brand experiences as a strategic communications channel. The practice covers trade shows and industry conferences, branded activations and pop-ups, flagship retail experiences, executive and customer summits, festival sponsorships, hospitality programs, and the production infrastructure that makes large-scale live events possible. The discipline overlaps with public relations, marketing, sponsorship, and the broader hospitality and production industries.

Trade shows have roots in the medieval European trade fairs and continued to function as commerce infrastructure through the industrial era. The modern industry-conference format formalized in the postwar period, with foundational gatherings including the Consumer Electronics Show (founded 1967), the National Restaurant Association show, and the rise of comparable trade events across most major sectors. The experiential marketing category emerged in the 2000s as brands invested more heavily in branded environments, pop-ups, and activations as standalone marketing channels. The pandemic disruption of 2020 through 2022 forced rapid adaptation toward virtual and hybrid formats. The subsequent return of in-person events has reset expectations around production quality, attendee experience, and the integration of digital and physical environments.

## How is the Market Changing?

## What is Investor Relations?

Investor relations (IR) is the discipline of managing an organization's communication with shareholders, analysts, capital markets, and the broader investing public. The practice covers earnings communications (quarterly results, guidance, conference calls), regulatory disclosure (10-K and 10-Q filings, 8-K event disclosure, proxy materials), analyst engagement (both sell-side and buy-side), investor day programs, M&A communications, activist investor response, IPO communications, and the increasingly significant work of communicating with retail investors and the broader online investing community.

The modern practice traces from the establishment of the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission in 1934 and the disclosure framework introduced under the Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934. Dedicated IR functions formalized at most large public companies through the 1960s and 1970s. Regulation Fair Disclosure (Reg FD), adopted by the SEC in 2000, restructured how companies could share material non-public information, prohibiting selective disclosure to favored analysts or investors. The shareholder activism era of the 2010s expanded the IR function's role in proxy fights and engagement with activist investors. The retail-investor wave of 2020 onward — driven by zero-commission brokerage, Robinhood, Reddit-based investment communities, and broader retail participation — added a new audience for IR communications. The current period adds ESG and sustainability disclosure, AI-driven investor research, and the increasing role of generative search in shareholder and analyst research.

## How is the Market Changing?

Four structural shifts. First, retail investor participation has continued at elevated levels relative to the pre-2020 baseline, requiring IR functions to address an audience that researches differently than traditional institutional investors. Second, ESG and sustainability disclosure has become a substantial component of IR work, with evolving regulatory frameworks in the U.S., the European Union, and other major markets. Third, activist investor engagement has expanded — both in volume and in the sophistication of activist campaigns — affecting how companies prepare for and respond to proxy contests. Fourth, AI-driven investor research has begun to influence buy-side and sell-side analyst workflows, and generative search interfaces are being used by retail investors and even some institutional analysts as part of early-stage research on companies and categories.

## Why Disclosure Quality Matters

The quality, clarity, and consistency of disclosure has a direct effect on cost of capital, analyst coverage, and investor trust. Companies with well-structured IR programs — clear guidance practices, transparent disclosure, accessible investor materials, and credible executive communication — generally attract broader analyst coverage and lower-volatility shareholder bases than companies with weaker IR functions. **What is Reg FD?**
Regulation Fair Disclosure (Reg FD) was adopted by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission in 2000. The regulation prohibits selective disclosure of material non-public information — meaning a public company that discloses material information to one analyst or investor must disclose the same information broadly through a public channel. Reg FD substantially restructured how IR functions operate, formalizing earnings calls, broadly distributed press releases, and structured investor communications as the primary disclosure vehicles.

**What does activist investor response look like?**
Activist investor engagement involves preparation (proxy defense planning, vulnerability assessment, board readiness), response (engagement strategy, communications to institutional and retail shareholders, regulatory filings), and resolution (settlement, proxy contest, or sustained engagement). The category has grown in both volume and sophistication, and dedicated activist-response practices now operate at most large financial communications firms.

**How do AI systems influence investor research?**
Generative search interfaces and AI-driven analyst research tools are being used at various points in the investor research workflow — by retail investors researching companies, by sell-side and buy-side analysts conducting category research, and by financial journalists.
